What is Phishing?

Phishing is one of the most deceptive forms of cybercrime — a digital trap designed to steal your trust and your information. Attackers disguise themselves as legitimate companies, friends, or services, luring you into clicking fake links or sharing personal details like passwords and credit card numbers. What looks real could be a cleverly crafted scam. Stay alert, think before you click, and protect yourself — because in the world of phishing, even one wrong move can open the door to danger.

TYPES OF ATTACKS

Email Phishing

The most common type — attackers send fake emails that look like they’re from trusted companies or institutions, asking you to click malicious links or share sensitive details.

Spear Phishing

A highly targeted attack aimed at a specific person or organization. Criminals gather personal details to make the message look authentic and convincing.

Website Spoofing

Fraudsters create fake websites that look identical to real ones — tricking you into entering your login credentials or financial information.

Whaling

Aimed at high-profile individuals like CEOs or executives, whaling attacks use personalized messages to access corporate data or authorize fraudulent payments.

Vishing

Attackers use phone calls or voice messages, pretending to be customer service agents or officials, to steal sensitive data like bank details or OTPs.

Smishing

Phishing through text messages that prompt you to click a malicious link, often disguised as a delivery update, bank alert, or prize notification.

MITIGATION

Think Before You Click

Always double-check links before opening them. Hover over URLs to see where they lead — if it looks suspicious or unfamiliar, don’t click.

Verify the Sender

Be cautious of unexpected emails or messages. Look closely at the sender’s email address — even small spelling differences can signal a fake.

Avoid Sharing Personal Information

Legitimate organizations never ask for passwords, PINs, or credit card details over email or text. If asked, it’s likely a scam.

Keep Software Updated & Use Security Tools

Outdated browsers or systems can be exploited by attackers. Always install security updates and patches on time.Enable spam filters, use antivirus software, and turn on multi-factor authentication (MFA). These add extra layers of protection.

Report Suspicious Emails

If you receive a phishing attempt, report it to your IT department, email provider, or local cyber authority. Reporting helps protect others too.

REPORT

If you received a phishing email

Do NOT click any links or download attachments. Then report it as follows:

To your email provider: Gmail: Open the email → click the three dots (⋮) → Report phishing. Outlook / Hotmail: Select the email → click Report → Phishing. Yahoo Mail: Open the email → click the three dots → Report phishing.

To government or official authorities.

If it was a text message

Do not reply or click any links.

Forward the message to your carrier’s spam reporting number: U.S. carriers: Forward to 7726 (SPAM) U.K.: Forward to 7726 as well

You can also report it to your country’s cyber authority (as above).

If it was on a website or social media

Report directly on the platform (e.g., Facebook, Instagram, X/Twitter → “Report” or “Mark as suspicious”).

You can also report fake websites to: Google Safe Browsing: https://safebrowsing.google.com/safebrowsing/report_phish/ Microsoft: https://www.microsoft.com/warningreport

If you entered personal or financial information

Change your passwords on affected accounts.

Enable 2FA (two-factor authentication).

Contact your bank or credit card company to block or monitor suspicious transactions.

Run antivirus software if you downloaded anything.
